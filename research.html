Research Projects

Shared Experiences, Connected Brains (2024.5-now)

This project investigates how shared co-viewing experiences and conversations shape the perception of social connection in the context of real-life social interaction. We first developed HyperStudy, a lab-based web-app platform for co-watching and other naturalistic interaction experiments, and then used fNIRS and fMRI hyperscanning to examine how the human social brain responds as pairs of participants watched a “Mole” guessing game and discussed their impressions in real time. Planned analyses focus on neural coupling, conversational alignment, and behavioral agreement, and how these mechanisms interact to support dyadic social connection.

Denoising in Naturalistic fMRI (2023.5–now) [Poster]

This project addresses the gap that most fMRI denoising methods were designed for task or resting-state data and are not optimized for naturalistic paradigms. We began with a large-scale review of 1,000+ naturalistic fMRI studies, using clustering analysis to identify common preprocessing strategies and evaluate their impact on data robustness. We then systematically tested denoising steps—including despiking, motion regression, high-pass filtering, and CSF regression—on a variety of analysis approaches while participants watched a 45-minute episode of Friday Night Lights, from the open-source dataset (OpenNeuro ds003521). Specifically, we examined their effects on temporal signals, spatiotemporal analyses, functional connectivity, and univariate models. We found that despiking and CSF regression increased temporal inter-subject correlations (ISC), while high-pass filtering disrupted slow neural dynamics in regions such as vmPFC and V1, despite sometimes enhancing functional connectivity. Together, these results revealed wide variability in preprocessing practices and demonstrated that preprocessing decisions are not neutral—they systematically shape outcomes across multiple naturalistic fMRI analysis methods. We recommend including despiking, motion parameters, and optionally CSF regressors, while avoiding high-pass filtering for naturalistic data.

Neural Stability Across Repeated Experiences (2022.9–now) [Poster] [Conference Manuscript]

This project investigates the neural stability of human perceptual and interpretive systems when individuals repeatedly experience the same naturalistic stimuli. Using fMRI, we scanned 20 participants as they watched 24 short videos across five sessions, allowing comparisons of neural similarity within and across individuals. Our findings revealed a clear dissociation: the visual cortex (V1) showed strong repetition effects, with neural responses becoming less consistent across repeated exposures, whereas the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) was more sensitive to contextual effects, with neural similarity shaped by the temporal proximity of viewings. Across brain regions, within-subject correlations were higher than between-subject correlations, yet session-to-session variability underscored the uniqueness of each experience. These results suggest that while sensory regions provide stable, stimulus-driven responses, higher-order association cortices flexibly integrate contextual and endogenous factors, highlighting how the brain transforms repeated events into unique experiences.

Narrative Transmission Across Individuals (2020–2024) [Manuscript]

This study aimed to investigate the neurocognitive mechanisms of narrative transmission, focusing on how episodic memory processes shape fidelity or distortion as stories pass between individuals. We innovatively applied a social transmission chain paradigm with fMRI, where 58 participants listened to and recalled stories across individuals. Topic modelling method allowed us to distinguish between schematic transmission (preserving narrative structure) and paraphrastic transmission (rephrasing content). Behaviorally, schematic transmission remained stable and convergent, whereas paraphrastic transmission produced distortion and divergence. Neural restuls showed, subsystems of the Default Mode Network (DMN) and hippocampal subregions formed a dynamic episodic memory system that supported transmission. These findings provide the first neuroimaging evidence of narrative transmission and highlight how episodic memory functions as a dynamic system, balancing stability and transformation with implications for information propagation, misinformation, and cultural continuity.
